COMP126: Practical Web Design & Development for Everyone

course schedule

Course description

COMP 126 provides a ground-up introduction to current principles, standards, and best practices in web site design, usability, accessibility, and general web development using HTML5, CSS3, very basic JavaScript, and a selection of other tools and frameworks commonly used in contemporary web design and development.

In fall 2021, COMP 380 will offer a blend of asynchronous and synchronous, remote, and in-person discussions, activities, and assignments.

Target audience

This course was designed to be useful in different ways to different students at different levels, from absolute beginners to more advanced programmers hoping to complement back-end web development skills with an understanding of the front-end design and implementation process. It welcomes and equally prioritizes the goals of not only aspiring professional web developers but artists, entrepreneurs, librarians, digital humanists, musicians, hobbyists, and everyone else. No prior technical expertise or programming experience is assumed or required.

What this course covers: The fundamentals of modern HTML5 and CSS3; desktop and mobile layout and design principles; web accessibility standards; and an introduction to basic usability, UI (user interface design), and UX (user experience design).

What this course does not cover: JavaScript (much); client-side or back-end programming; databases; full-stack frameworks such as React, Vue, or Angular.

CS major/minors, experienced programmers, and all those with web development skills please note!: You are welcome to use this course as a place to sharpen your HTML and CSS skills and learn a few things about design. However, please be aware that this is an HTML and CSS fundamentals course. It is not an introduction to the types of front-end frameworks (e.g., React, Angular, etc.) that many of you will encounter in industry. And it does not count toward the major or minor in Computer Science.

Once again, for emphasis: this course does not count toward the major BS, BA, or minor curriculum in Computer Science.

  

Goals & objectives

The primary goal of this course is to help students become competent advanced-beginner- to intermediate-level web designers, independent learners, and capable problem solvers through a project-based approach to responsive web and UI design and development and its associated troubleshooting strategies. To this end, our primary objectives and planned learning outcomes are as follows.

By the end of this course, students will:

  • Possess an understanding of the basic and necessary processes and skills involved in front-end responsive web design and development;
  • Possess a working HTML5 and CSS3 skillset;
  • Understand and be able to employ modern UI design principles;
  • Possess the knowledge necessary to effectively pursue further formal or self-motivated education in more advanced web development tools and skills;
  • Have developed a well-designed, well-functioning live web site suitable for inclusion in, or as, a professional, artistic, and/or academic portfolio.
  

Final project

In the COMP 126 final project, you'll form teams of 2-4 people and operate as a web design and development team hired to build a site for a client. I encourage you to find a “real” client for a “real” project among yourselves or in the community: an individual, group, club, company, whatever. The important thing is to have a way to define a client’s needs, goals, and priorities for the design and build of the site; why not offer your web development services to a group in need?

Quizzes, homework, & exams

Unless specified otherwise, homework and other short assignments will be scored on a 0-2 scale, as follows:

0: Hasn't been scored yet; wasn't completed and/or properly submitted in the first place; or the was obviously completed with an exceptionally and unacceptably low level of effort, understanding, and/or acquaintance with the readings/topics.

1: Was completed on time but does not fully meet the requirements of the assignment. This usually means that submission is thin, general, incomplete, and/or lacks specific reference to and engagement with the course materials and concepts.

2: Completed on time, thoroughly, and in good faith, with evidence of effort and clear engagement with the assigned prompt, reading, viewing, question/s, and/or task/s.

Quizzes will usually comprise 10 multiple-choice questions and be delivered on Sakai with a 30-minute time limit, open book, open notes, open everything. (The professor reserves the right to alter this format; notice will be given of any changes.) Quiz make-ups will only be granted when accompanied by a University excused absence or when arranged and approved by the professor at least 24 hours in advance. We do not drop a lowest quiz score.

There will be a midterm exam and a final exam. The midterm will be half multiple-choice, half a coding exercise in which you'll be given an image of a website layout you must re-create using HTML and CSS as directed. The final exam will be multiple-choice only, since your final project—a fully developed, live website—is due at the same time and covers the coding aspect.

The final exam will be administered in compliance with UNC's final exam regulations and scheduled according to the UNC Final Exam calendar.

Quiz and midterm make-ups/reschedulings will only be granted when accompanied by a University excused absence or when arranged and approved by the professor at least 24 hours in advance. Final exam make-up/rescheduling requires a final exam excuse from the Dean's Office.


Late assignments & extensions

In this class, we accept late assignments for credit (with 0.25 late penalty) for two weeks after each assignment's original due date, and only up until the last scheduled day of class.

Requests for reasonable extensions will be considered when requested in advance—preferably 24 but at least 12 hours before the assignment is due. However, for group projects, all group members must agree to request the extension and submit the request jointly.

For late assignments that are part of the final project, an immediate penalty of three points (out of 100, in the case of these assignments) will be applied as soon as the assignment is late, then another point for every two hours thereafter.

Quiz and midterm make-ups/reschedulings will only be granted when accompanied by a University excused absence or when arranged and approved by the professor at least 24 hours in advance. Final exam make-up/rescheduling requires a final exam excuse from the Dean's Office.

Honor code

Every time you turn in an assignment at this University, you implicitly agree—whether or not you sign anything—that you have neither given nor received unauthorized aid, that the work you present is your own, and that you’ve properly cited and attributed all source materials.

Plagiarism is an especially serious breach of UNC’s Honor Code and of your own intellectual and personal integrity. It includes any use of another’s words, ideas, or research—whether quoted, paraphrased, or mentioned—without proper and full citation and attribution. This applies to materials taken from any source, online or off, including code.

Note that assignment/exam submissions containing code fully or primarily authored by someone else will also be considered plagiarism, even if that code was offered freely by its author for use and re-use, and even if you cite your source. The code you write in this class must be primarily your own original work. More details on how much code you’re permitted to borrow in this class (and why) are available here

.

Please also note that although collaboration with other COMP 126 students may be permitted on certain assignments, it is absolutely prohibited on any aspect of any quiz or exam.

If you do not understand the definitions of plagiarism, citation, attribution, or “unauthorized aid”, it is your responsibility to remedy that; ignorance will not be accepted as an excuse. UNC Libraries' plagiarism tutorial is a good place to start: https://guides.lib.unc.edu/plagiarism/purpose

Please feel free to contact me with any questions or for additional clarification.

Because it is neither my responsibility nor my place to determine whether breaches of the Honor Code are intentional or unintentional, I will immediately report all suspected violations to the Honor Court, which may result in a failing grade, probation, suspension, or expulsion.
